LOS 2.a: Calculate and interpret the present value (PV) of fixed-income and equity instruments based on expected future cash flows

The present value (PV) is the current worth of a future sum of money or stream of cash flows given a specific rate of return.
The future value (FV) represents how much a present sum will grow to in the future, assuming it earns a constant return rate.

Relationship Between Present and Future Value

\[ FV = PV(1 + r)^t \]

\[ PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^t} = FV(1 + r)^{-t} \]

where:
- \(r\) = interest rate per compounding period
- \(t\) = number of compounding periods

If we are using continuous compounding, the relationship becomes:

\[ FV = PV \times e^{rt} \]

\[ PV = FV \times e^{-rt} \]

Fixed-Income Securities

A pure discount debt instrument, such as a zero-coupon bond, provides a simple example of the time value of money.
The investor pays less than the face value to buy the instrument and receives the face value at maturity.
The price depends on the yield to maturity (YTM) and time to maturity.
The interest earned is the difference between the face value and purchase price.

Example — Zero-Coupon Bond

A zero-coupon bond with a face value of $1,000 will mature 15 years from today.
The bond has a yield to maturity of 4% with annual compounding.
What is the bond’s price?

\[ PV = \frac{1,000}{(1 + 0.04)^{15}} = \$555.26 \]

Finding Yield from Price (BA II Plus)

If we are given the price of $555.26, the face value of $1,000, and annual compounding over 15 years, enter:

Key Value
PV –555.26
FV 1,000
PMT 0
N 15
CPT → I/Y 4.00%

Result: The bond’s yield to maturity (YTM) is 4.00%.

Professor’s Note:
Enter cash outflows as negative values and cash inflows as positive values.
From the investor’s perspective, PV (purchase price) is an outflow, and FV (face value) is an inflow.

Negative Interest Rates

In some circumstances, interest rates can be negative.
A zero-coupon bond with a negative yield will be priced at a premium (price > face value).

Example — Zero-Coupon Bond with Negative Yield

If the bond in the previous example has a yield to maturity of –0.5%, with annual compounding, the price is:

\[ PV = \frac{1,000}{(1 - 0.005)^{15}} = \$1,078.09 \]

Fixed-Coupon Bonds

A fixed-coupon bond provides periodic coupon payments in addition to the face value at maturity.
The coupon rate determines the coupon payment, while the yield to maturity (YTM) determines the discount rate used for valuation.

For example, a 3% annual coupon, $1,000 par bond pays
\[30 = 3\% \times 1,000\]
each year.

Note:
The coupon rate determines PMT, not the discount rate.
The YTM (I/Y) is the rate implied by the bond’s price.

Example — Annual Coupon Bond

Consider a 10-year, $1,000 par, 10% annual coupon bond.
What is the value of this bond if its yield to maturity is 8%?

Step 1: Compute annual cash flows.
Coupon payments = 10% × $1,000 = $100 per year.

Step 2: Discount all cash flows to present value.

\[ PV = \frac{100}{1.08} + \frac{100}{(1.08)^2} + \frac{100}{(1.08)^3} + \dots + \frac{100}{(1.08)^9} + \frac{1,100}{(1.08)^{10}} \]

\[ PV = \$1,134.20 \]

BA II Plus Solution

Key Value
N 10
PMT 100
FV 1,000
I/Y 8
CPT → PV –1,134.20

Result: The bond’s value is $1,134.20.

Professor’s Note

For this reading, where we want to illustrate time value of money concepts, we are only using annual coupon payments and compounding periods.
In the Fixed Income topic area, we will also perform these calculations for semiannual-pay bonds.

Perpetuities

Some bonds have no maturity date.
We refer to these as perpetual bonds or perpetuities.
Because perpetuities never mature, we cannot define a meaningful future value, but their present value simplifies mathematically to:

\[ PV_{\text{perpetuity}} = \frac{\text{payment}}{r} \]

Amortizing Bonds and Annuities

An amortizing bond pays a level amount each period, including its final maturity payment.
Each payment includes both interest and principal, unlike a fixed-coupon bond, which returns the entire principal at maturity.

Amortizing bonds are examples of annuity instruments.
For an annuity, the periodic payment can be calculated as:

\[ \text{Annuity Payment} = \frac{r \times PV}{1 - (1 + r)^{-t}} \]

where:
- \(r\) = interest rate per period
- \(t\) = number of periods
- \(PV\) = present value (principal)

Example — Computing a Loan Payment

Suppose you are considering applying for a $2,000 loan that will be repaid with equal end-of-year payments over the next 13 years.
If the annual interest rate is 6%, what is the size of each payment?

We can compute the payment (PMT) using the given values.
Since the loan will be fully repaid, FV = 0.

Key Value
N 13
I/Y 6
PV –2,000
FV 0
CPT → PMT 225.92

Result: The end-of-year payment is $225.92.

Equity Securities

Just as with fixed-income securities, we value equity securities (such as common and preferred stock) as the present value of their expected future cash flows.
However, equity securities differ in two important ways:
1. They do not mature.
2. Their cash flows may change over time.

Preferred Stock Valuation

Preferred stock pays a fixed dividend, usually stated as a percentage of par value.
The investor’s required return is the discount rate used to value the stock.

Because preferred dividends are fixed and indefinite, we can use the perpetuity formula:

\[ V_{\text{preferred}} = \frac{D_p}{k_p} \]

where:
- \(D_p\) = dividend per period
- \(k_p\) = required return on the preferred stock

Example — Preferred Stock Valuation

A company’s $100 par preferred stock pays a $5 annual dividend and has a required return of 8%.
Calculate the value of the preferred stock.

\[ V_{\text{preferred}} = \frac{5.00}{0.08} = \$62.50 \]

Result: The preferred stock’s value is $62.50.

Common Stock Valuation

Common stock represents a residual claim on a company’s assets after all obligations are met.
Unlike preferred stock, it does not promise fixed dividends — the company’s management decides if and when dividends are paid.

Because cash flows are uncertain, analysts use dividend discount models (DDMs) to estimate value.
We focus on three key versions:

  1. Constant Dividend (Perpetuity) — dividends are constant forever.
  2. Constant Growth (Gordon Growth Model) — dividends grow at a constant rate.
  3. Multistage Growth — dividends grow at varying rates before stabilizing.

1. Constant Dividend (Perpetuity)

If dividends remain constant forever, we use the perpetuity formula:

\[ V_0 = \frac{D}{k_e} \]

2. Constant Growth Model (Gordon Growth Model)

If dividends are expected to grow at a constant rate, the value of a stock is:

\[ V_0 = \frac{D_1}{k_e - g_c} \]

where:
- \(V_0\) = current value of the stock
- \(D_1\) = dividend expected next period
- \(k_e\) = required return on equity
- \(g_c\) = constant growth rate of dividends

Note: \(k_e\) must be greater than \(g_c\) for the model to work.

Example — Gordon Growth Model Valuation

Calculate the value of a stock expected to pay a $1.62 dividend next year, with dividends growing at 8% forever, and a required return of 12%.

\[ V_0 = \frac{1.62}{0.12 - 0.08} = \$40.50 \]

Result: The stock’s value is $40.50.

3. Multistage Dividend Discount Model (DDM)

If a stock experiences changing dividend growth, we use the multistage DDM.
This assumes high short-term growth followed by constant long-term growth.

Steps:
1. Estimate short-term dividends individually.
2. Apply the constant growth model starting from the first dividend that grows at the constant rate.
3. Discount all cash flows back to present value.

Example — Multistage Growth

A stock’s dividends are expected to grow at 15% per year for two years, then 5% per year indefinitely.
The last dividend paid (D₀) was $1.00, and the required return (kₑ) is 11%.
Find the value of the stock.

Step 1 — Compute high-growth dividends

\[ D_1 = D_0(1 + g^*) = 1.00(1.15) = 1.15 \]

\[ D_2 = D_1(1 + g^*) = 1.15(1.15) = 1.32 \]

Step 2 — Find terminal value (P₁) using the constant growth model from D₂ onward:

\[ P_1 = \frac{D_2}{k_e - g_c} = \frac{1.32}{0.11 - 0.05} = 22.00 \]

Step 3 — Discount D₁ and P₁ back to present

\[ V_0 = \frac{1.15 + 22.00}{1.11} = \$20.86 \]

Result: The value of the stock today is $20.86.

Professor’s Note

When we apply the dividend in Period 2 to the constant growth model, it yields a value for the stock in Period 1.
To obtain the current value (V₀), we must discount that value back one period, along with any earlier dividends not included in the constant growth portion.
